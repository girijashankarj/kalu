## 0.0 Intent, Scope, and Ethical Position [Context]

This biography is a record of Kalu's life, the bond we shared with him, and the critical-care journey that followed his accident in December 2025.

Kalu's life markers:
- Birthdate: 26 Nov 2016
- Lived: 9 years
- Last day: 16 Dec 2025

This record is not written to harass individuals or make allegations. It focuses on systems, communication, preparedness, and what pet parents should know before emergencies. Where facts are disputed, we present Our Account, Clinic Response, and Our Clarification separately.

Kalu was in a critical, life-threatening condition; survival was not guaranteed. We accept that he may not have survived regardless of location. The concern documented here is not outcome, but communication, preparedness, empathy, and systemic bottlenecks in emergency veterinary care.

This is not a call for boycott or retaliation. Not a medical claim or clinical verdict. Not defamation or an accusation of intent. Not a replacement for professional veterinary advice. All medical events are described from the perspective of lived experience, not as expert diagnosis.

Kalu was family. Loved. Fought for. Never forgotten.

## 1.0 Before He Had a Name [Nov 2016]

### 1.1 The Ordinary Night That Stopped Being Ordinary [Nov 2016]
I did not plan to bring a dog home that night. There was no thought of responsibility, no calculation of money, time, or future. I was not emotionally prepared for a life to depend on me. I was simply standing on a street, late at night, on a work call, trying to finish what the day demanded from me.

That is how ordinary moments begin until they stop being ordinary.

It was the last week of November 2016. The street was familiar. Nothing about the night felt special. Then I noticed movement that did not belong to the background noise of the city. A few dogs were circling something smaller. At first glance, it did not look dramatic. Dogs gather all the time. But this was different. Their movement was not playful. It was not curious. It was calculated. They were closing space, tightening distance, waiting for an opening.

The puppy in the middle was small. Too small to defend himself, too small to understand what was happening. He was not barking. He was not running properly. He was frozen in confusion.

I did not think. I did not weigh consequences. I picked him up.

I continued my work call while holding him. The call lasted almost another hour. During that entire time, the puppy stayed with me, on my lap, climbing, licking, playing, as if he had already decided that this was safe now.

The bigger dogs did not leave. They stayed at a distance, watching, waiting for me to move away, waiting for me to put the puppy down. That is when it became clear to me: If I walked away, even for a moment, the puppy would not survive the night.

There was no dramatic rescue scene. No cheering. No help from anyone nearby. It was just a quiet decision made in the middle of a normal street.

I brought the puppy into our society parking. I locked the gate. At that moment, I still believed this was temporary. I assumed that by morning, the puppy would go away on his own, find his way, disappear back into the randomness of the city. I went upstairs.

That night passed without ceremony. No celebration. No naming. No declarations of forever. Just a puppy locked safely away from death.

### 1.2 The Morning That Changed Everything [Nov 2016]
The next morning, I went down to the parking. I did not call his name. He did not have one yet. I made a small sound, instinctive, meaningless: "चुक चुक."

From the first floor, I heard movement. The puppy came down the stairs. Not slowly. Not hesitantly. He ran. He ran as if he already knew me.

I picked him up. He licked my ears. That was not affection learned over time. That was trust given instantly.

In that moment, something irreversible happened, even if I did not yet understand it.

I went upstairs and asked my father a simple question: "Should I keep him?"

My father said yes. There was no long discussion. No list of reasons. No warnings. Just agreement. The puppy stayed.

That puppy would later be called Kalu.

### 1.3 The House He Walked Into [Nov 2016]
When Kalu entered our home, the house was already carrying weight.

My father was not well, not temporarily, not mildly. Three years earlier, he had met with a serious accident on the Hadapsar bridge while riding a two-wheeler. The damage was neurological. One leg was partially paralysed. His vision was affected in both eyes. He could not see clearly anymore, not consistently, not reliably.

This kind of injury does not announce itself loudly. It settles into daily life. Movement becomes cautious. Confidence becomes fragile. Independence slowly slips away.

Our flat was on the ground floor, with a decent garden area. That garden became part of Kalu's early world, and also a quiet extension of my father's limited mobility.

My sister and I went to college. We were away for long hours. During that time, my father stayed home alone. Not alone in the sense of absence of people, but alone in the sense that the world had become harder to navigate.

Kalu grew up inside this environment. Not as a distraction. Not as entertainment. But as presence. He stayed close to my father without being taught to do so. He learned where to position himself so my father would not trip. He sensed when to move and when to stay still. He did not demand attention. He did not create chaos. He simply existed where he was needed.

People in the society began to complain. Some people are genuinely scared of dogs. That fear is real, and I do not deny it. But many complaints came even when Kalu was not near anyone. Some people wanted trouble. Arguments happened. Stress increased.

What those people never saw was what happened inside our home. Kalu was not adding burden. He was absorbing it. The house did not collapse during those months, not because life was easy, but because something steady stayed inside it.

### 1.4 He Did Not Save Us, He Stabilised Us [2016]
Kalu did not cure my father. He did not fix paralysis. He did not restore vision. But he did something quieter and harder to notice. He made the house bearable. He occupied silence so it did not turn into despair. He became something predictable in a life that had lost predictability.

At that time, none of us said this out loud. We did not know yet how important it was.

## 2.0 Learning to Live With Each Other [2016-2017]

### 2.1 Adjusting Without a Manual [2016-2017]
Kalu did not arrive as a trained dog. He arrived as a question.

Every day after that morning in November 2016 became an adjustment, not just for him, but for us. There was no manual, no transition period where things slowly fell into place. Life continued at its pace, and Kalu learned to move inside it.

Our flat was on the ground floor, with a garden that opened directly into the outside world. That garden became Kalu's first real territory. Not ownership, familiarity. He learned the smell of the soil. The pattern of birds. The timing of footsteps. The difference between morning quiet and evening noise. He learned without instruction.

My sister and I were still going to college. Most of the day, the house belonged to my father and Kalu. This was not companionship in a dramatic sense. It was not playing all day or constant interaction. It was quieter than that.

Kalu stayed close. He would lie where my father could sense him. Not necessarily touching, just present. Always within awareness. If my father shifted in his chair, Kalu shifted. If my father rested, Kalu settled. If my father moved slowly, Kalu gave him space. There was an intelligence in this that no one taught him.

### 2.2 The Weight of Being Seen by Others [2016-2017]
Outside our home, Kalu's presence was not always welcomed. Some people in the society were scared of dogs. I understood that. Fear is instinctive, and it does not always need justification. But there were others who were not afraid, they were uncomfortable. Uncomfortable with a dog in shared space. Uncomfortable with a family already dealing with disability. Uncomfortable with something they could not control.

Complaints started coming in. Not because Kalu had bitten anyone. Not because he chased or attacked. Sometimes he was not even near the people complaining. Arguments happened. Tension rose. It added another layer of stress to a house that already had enough.

Kalu did not react to this chaos. He did not become aggressive. He did not grow anxious. If anything, he became more contained. He learned where to sit. Where not to cross. When to be visible and when to disappear into corners. He was not shrinking himself out of fear. He was adapting.

### 2.3 Food, Routine, and Small Preferences [2016-2017]
Kalu's food was simple, almost basic. Milk. Chapati. Maida pav. Sugary things he should not have liked but did. Raw vegetables in his early days. There was nothing curated or fancy about his meals.

He loved chicken tandoor from early on. That was obvious. But I am vegetarian, so he did not get it often. Mostly weekends. He accepted that without protest. Food never became a point of conflict. He ate what was given. He enjoyed what he could.

Baths, however, were a different story. Kalu hated bath time. The word "अंघोळ" alone could change his mood. He resisted water when it was forced on him. He avoided tubs, buckets, and attempts to clean him on command.

And yet, rain walks were his joy. When it rained, and we went out for a walk, he loved getting wet naturally. He enjoyed rain on his body when it was part of movement, freedom, choice. That contradiction stayed with him all his life.

### 2.4 Freedom Without Chaos [2016-2017]
Kalu was never chained. He was never tied to one location. Never restricted to a corner.

He moved freely inside the house and garden. Sometimes, when allowed, he went outside. There was an empty ground in front of our society then. He would go there, do his chores, and return on his own.

No calling. No chasing.

He learned the boundaries of home not through restriction, but through repetition. Later, he learned chain walking too. But that was a skill, not a requirement for obedience. He understood space intuitively.

### 2.5 Language Before Commands [2016-2017]
Kalu did not learn English commands. He learned Marathi.

He responded clearly to: "चला", "खायचे", "बाहेर जायचे". These were not commands shouted for control. They were shared language.

Some words he disliked deeply. "अंघोळ". "Kame Hame Haa" - something I used to say playfully while shaking him, from Dragon Ball Z. "मारू का". "सरक".

He reacted to tone, not just words. He understood intent.

### 2.6 Rough Play and Honest Boundaries [2016-2017]
Kalu's play was not soft. He liked running behind us in the garden. Chasing legs. Jumping suddenly. Playing hide-and-seek. He did not care for toys. He ignored balls, ropes, anything artificial. He loved wooden sticks. He loved rugs, grabbing, pulling, testing strength.

Sometimes play turned aggressive. Across his life, he bit only me, once or twice in those early meeting days, and later occasionally when play crossed a line or on days I still do not fully understand. I never hurt him.

In his first two or three walks, I used a stick briefly for discipline. Not out of anger. Out of inexperience. He learned quickly. After that, we never needed it again. There was no pattern of fear. There was no cycle of violence. There was learning.

### 2.7 The Quiet Bond That No One Noticed [2016-2017]
While all this was happening, college, complaints, routines, something deeper was forming quietly.

Kalu was growing alongside my father. He was not replacing anyone. He was not filling a role. But when the house felt fragile, he stayed steady. When voices were low, he did not add noise. When days were heavy, he did not demand energy. He learned when to be visible and when to disappear into stillness.

At the time, I did not fully see this. It only made sense later.

### 2.8 June 2017 Came Without Warning [Jun 2017]
In June 2017, my father passed away. There was no gradual preparation for that loss. One day, he was there. The next, he was not.

## 3.0 After One Pillar Left, and Life Did Not Stop [2017-2019]

### 3.1 The House Without a Centre [2017-2019]
When my father passed away in June 2017, nothing announced itself loudly. There was no dramatic collapse of routine. No visible breaking point where the house fell apart. Life simply continued without him. That is what made it harder.

The house did not scream. It went quiet. The kind of quiet that does not ask for attention but slowly takes up space.

My sister and I were suddenly responsible for more than we were ready for. Responsibilities did not arrive in a list. They arrived as weight, distributed unevenly across days.

By December 2017, my office life intensified. Longer hours. More pressure. Less presence at home. This was not a choice I debated emotionally. It was survival. Bills did not pause for grief. Work did not slow down because someone had died.

Kalu adjusted again.

### 3.2 Reduced Time, Increased Distance, Without Abandonment [2017-2019]
As my work hours increased, I spent less time at home. That guilt never left. It stayed in the background, never loud enough to paralyse me, but never quiet enough to ignore.

Kalu did not react with resentment. He did not destroy things. He did not become needy. He did not withdraw. He simply recalibrated. He learned the rhythms of absence. He understood when mornings were rushed and evenings were exhausted. He adapted to the reality of someone coming home tired rather than available. This was not obedience. This was intelligence.

### 3.3 Growing Without Guidance, Yet Not Lost [2017-2019]
Kalu was growing physically now. Stronger. More aware of his size. More confident in movement. He was not formally trained. There were no classes. No obedience drills.

And yet, he learned boundaries. He learned which areas were his. Which were shared. Which required caution. He knew when to be visible and when to retreat into corners of familiarity. He remained free.

He still went out occasionally on his own, did his chores, and came back. The empty ground in front of the society was still there then. He knew the route. He knew the return. There was trust both ways.

### 3.4 Society, Again [2017-2019]
Complaints did not stop after my father's passing. In some ways, they increased. Perhaps because vulnerability invites scrutiny. Perhaps because a family without an elder becomes easier to pressure. Perhaps because people sensed there was less resistance.

Arguments happened. Tension flared. Kalu remained unaffected. He did not react to raised voices. He did not mirror aggression. If anything, he became more observant. He started recognising not just people, but intent. He knew which footsteps were familiar. Which bikes belonged to people he trusted. Which voices signalled comfort.

From inside the flat, he could correctly identify arrivals, friends, familiar visitors, family, before we opened the door. He would start wagging his tail early, certain of who was coming. This ability never felt like a trick. It felt like awareness sharpened by attention.

### 3.5 A Brother, Not a Responsibility [2017-2019]
At some point during these years, something subtle but important shifted in me.

I stopped thinking of Kalu as my dog. He became something closer to a brother. Not in sentiment, but in dynamic. We shared space without explanation. We coexisted without negotiation. We tolerated each other's moods. He did not demand affection. I did not perform care. We simply lived together.

### 3.6 The Days That Were Just Days [2017-2019]
There were no major milestones during this period. No dramatic stories. No defining incidents. Just days. Days where Kalu slept in familiar spots. Days where he watched birds from the garden. Days where he chased cats along the wall. Days where he played rough, ran, stopped suddenly, lay down. Days where nothing happened and everything was stable.

Looking back, these were the strongest years. Because nothing needed fixing.

### 3.7 The Silence That Stayed [2017-2019]
Even as time moved forward, my father's absence did not fade. It became background. Not painful every moment. But present always.

Kalu did not fill that absence. He acknowledged it. He lay in spaces my father used to occupy. He stayed near where silence felt heaviest. He did not replace anyone. He made it possible to live with the absence without being consumed by it.

### 3.8 The World Was About to Change [Late 2019]
By late 2019, something else was approaching, something that would slow time for everyone. The world would pause. And for the first time in years, I would be home again, not out of choice, but circumstance.

That is where Kalu's life entered its next phase.

## 4.0 When the World Stopped, and Time Came Back [2019-2022]

### 4.1 Time Returned, But Not as Peace [2019-2022]
When COVID arrived, it did not knock. It shut doors. Offices closed. Roads emptied. Plans dissolved overnight. What had always felt permanent suddenly became uncertain. For the first time in years, the world slowed down, not by choice, but by force.

And suddenly, I was home. Not for a weekend. Not for a few days. Every day. For a long time.

At first, it did not feel like relief. There was anxiety everywhere. News. Numbers. Fear. Isolation. The house was quiet again, but this time it was a different kind of quiet.

My friends started coming over once restrictions eased. Slowly. Carefully. They filled the house in a way it had not been filled since my father passed away.

Kalu noticed immediately. He did not retreat from new presence. He did not become territorial. He observed.

### 4.2 Kalu and People, Not Dogs [2019-2022]
Kalu had always preferred humans over dogs. That did not change. During this period, it became clearer.

He did not rush toward people. He did not jump or demand affection. He watched first. Then he decided. Once accepted, he remembered.

He remembered voices. Footsteps. The way people laughed. The way they sat. The way they moved around the house. He adjusted himself around them without being intrusive. Friends noticed this. "He understands," some of them said. And they were right.

He did not seek constant interaction. He stayed nearby. Present, but not needy. When people sat, he sat. When conversations slowed, he settled. When laughter rose, he watched.

### 4.3 The House Felt Alive Again [2019-2022]
For the first time since 2017, the house felt occupied, not just inhabited. Friends came and stayed longer. Meals stretched into conversations. Silence was shared instead of avoided.

Kalu moved through this naturally. He did not guard. He did not withdraw. He flowed. If someone stayed overnight, Kalu knew. If someone left suddenly, Kalu noticed. He did not panic at change, he tracked it.

### 4.4 Play, Roughness, and Familiar Chaos [2019-2022]
With more people around, play returned. Not structured play. Not planned. Spontaneous.

Kalu ran behind legs in the garden. Chased us. Stopped suddenly. Changed direction without warning. He played hide-and-seek between trees, behind walls, behind furniture.

Sometimes play turned rough. That had always been his style. He did not know gentle play the way toy-trained dogs do. He knew honest engagement. Strength testing. Presence testing.

He never bit strangers. Never troubled kids. He tolerated crowds of 30 to 40 people, including small children, without aggression. He remained selective, but never unsafe.

### 4.5 Learning Without Training [2019-2022]
This period highlighted something important. Kalu learned not because he was taught, but because he was observing constantly. He picked up routines. He knew when food was coming. He knew when night walks were likely. He knew when the house was settling down.

Words mattered less than patterns. He understood Marathi cues naturally, but more than that, he understood context. Tone. Timing. Intent. That was his real language.

### 4.6 Vehicles, Revisited [2019-2022]
Earlier attempts to train Kalu on two-wheelers had failed. We tried putting him on an Activa. He did not like it. He never learned. He resisted without panic, but with clarity. This was not fear. It was rejection.

Later, with help from friends, we tried again, this time with the Maruti Alto. At first, he was hesitant. Not anxious. Not trembling. Just uncertain. We did not force it. Gradually, he sat. Then stayed. Then watched the road.

Over time, he began tolerating car rides. Eventually, he began enjoying them. Not excitement. Comfort.

### 4.7 Freedom, Still Intact [2019-2022]
Despite lockdowns and restrictions, Kalu's freedom was not crushed. He still moved between house and garden freely. He still chose where to sit. He still preferred clean places, bed, sofa, never mats. He still hated bath time. He still loved rain walks. Mud, garden digging, bird watching, nothing changed. The world outside was uncertain, but his world remained familiar.

### 4.8 The Quiet Bond Strengthened [2019-2022]
During this phase, something else happened quietly. My bond with Kalu changed. Earlier years were about survival. Adjustment. Holding things together. Now, it was about presence.

We were both there. Fully. Daily. No rushing out. No delayed returns. We shared space without urgency. He did not fill my time. He accompanied it.

### 4.9 Nothing Felt Final Yet [2019-2022]
Looking back now, it is tempting to treat these years as a prelude. But at the time, there was no sense of before and after.

## 5.0 When Family Changed Shape [2022-Early 2025]

### 5.1 The Return of Absence, But Different [2022-2025]
When the world began opening up after COVID, life did not return to what it was before. It rearranged itself. Work resumed. Movement returned. Days became structured again. The house that had been full during lockdown slowly returned to quieter rhythms.

Kalu noticed this shift immediately. He always did.

Earlier absences had felt heavy. They had carried grief. This time, absence felt more practical. People went out because life was moving again, not because something had been lost. Kalu adjusted without resistance. He no longer needed to hold the house together. He no longer needed to compensate for silence. He simply lived.

### 5.2 February 2023, Marriage and a New Centre of Gravity [Feb 2023]
In February 2023, I got married. This was not a sudden event for Kalu. He had sensed change long before the wedding day arrived. He watched preparations. He noticed new routines. He felt shifts in attention, movement, and tone.

When my wife entered the house fully, Kalu made a decision of his own. He chose her. Not gradually. Not hesitantly. Completely.

### 5.3 The Mother-Son Bond [2023-2025]
Kalu's bond with my wife was different from his bond with anyone else. It was immediate, instinctive, and deeply emotional. He followed her everywhere. Room to room. Kitchen to bedroom. Inside to outside. He did not leave her alone. Not out of anxiety. Out of attachment. It was not dependency. It was belonging.

If she sat, he sat nearby. If she moved, he followed. If she left the house, he waited differently. There was a tenderness in his behaviour that was unmistakable. It was a mother-son relationship. Pure. Unquestioned. Absolute.

### 5.4 Where I Stood in That Shift [2023-2025]
For me, Kalu remained a brother. That did not change. But I watched his emotional centre move, and I did not resent it. I understood it.

Kalu had always chosen the person who felt most constant in that phase of life. Earlier, that had been my father. Then the house itself. Then me, during lockdown. Now, it was my wife. This was not loss. It was growth.

### 5.5 A Fuller Home, A Calmer Dog [2023-2025]
With marriage came a different kind of stability. The house felt occupied again, not by crowds, but by continuity.

Kalu responded to this calm. He became more settled. More relaxed. More certain of routine. He still played rough. Still ran in the garden. Still chased cats. Still hated bath time. But there was less restlessness. He had found his person.

### 5.6 Night Walks, His Greatest Joy [2022-2025]
Through all changes, one thing never shifted. Kalu loved night walks. Late nights. Quiet roads. Familiar smells. Sometimes with us. Sometimes alone. He would ask to go out after 9 PM, then again at 12 AM, sometimes even 3 AM. Short walks. Purposeful walks. He would go. He would return. Always.

Those walks were not exercise. They were freedom rituals.

### 5.7 The City Changed, Kalu Adapted [2022-2025]
As time passed, the empty ground near our society was built over. Space reduced. Kalu adapted again. He began walking almost 100 meters to the main road to do his chores, then returned home. No confusion. No getting lost. He knew his route.

The city had changed around him, but he did not lose his sense of home.

### 5.8 Comforts, Small and Personal [2022-2025]
Kalu's preferences remained simple. He still loved sugary treats, milky items, pedha. He still preferred sitting where we sat, bed or sofa, never mats. He still enjoyed rain walks while hating baths. He still rejected anything forced and accepted anything chosen.

### 5.9 The New Car, The New Routine [2022-2025]
As my financial condition improved, I bought a new car. Kalu liked it. Not excitedly. Comfortably. He sat inside calmly. Observed. Trusted. It was another sign of stability, not indulgence.

### 5.10 Health, Strength, and No Warnings [Early 2025]
By early 2025, Kalu was healthy. Alert. Strong. Aware. There were no signs of decline.

### 6.5 Space, Dirt, and Clean Choices [2016-2025]

One of Kalu's favourite things was sitting in the back garden. Watching birds. Tracking movement.

When cats appeared on the garden wall, he reacted instantly, barking, chasing, alert and alive. This was not obsession. It was engagement.

He used to enjoy back scratches and belly rubs from all humans.

## 7.0 The Night Everything Broke [11 Dec 2025]

### 7.1 Routine, Not Risk [11 Dec 2025]
The day of 11 December 2025 did not feel unusual. There were no signs. No strange behaviour. No instinct warning us to hold him back. Kalu ate. He rested. He moved around the house the way he always did. Night came the way it always had.

Around 9:30 PM, Kalu asked to go out. This was normal. Night walks were his greatest joy. He did not rush. He did not sneak out. He asked through presence, movement, familiarity. We trusted that routine because it had never betrayed us before.

He had done this countless times. He knew the route. He knew the return. We let him go. There was no sense of danger. No hesitation. Nothing felt wrong.

### 7.2 The Call That Should Never Exist [11 Dec 2025]
Some time later, between 9:30 PM and 10:00 PM, my phone rang. An unknown number. I answered without urgency. The voice on the other side said something that took a moment to register: "कालू का एक्सिडेंट हुआ है।"

For a second, it did not make sense. Accident was a word that belonged to roads, vehicles, strangers. Not to Kalu. Not to routine. Not to a night that had started normally.

Then my body reacted before my mind did. I ran.

### 7.3 The Place Where It All Began, Again [11 Dec 2025]
When we reached the spot, the first thing that hit me was recognition. This was the same place where I had found Kalu as a puppy in 2016. The same stretch. The same road. The beginning and the breaking happened at the same location.

People were standing around. Around 20 to 30 people. They spoke in fragments. Someone said a biker hit him. Someone said the biker was drunk. Someone said the biker was speeding. They said the biker and another person fled. No one had stopped them. No one had taken Kalu to a hospital.

### 7.4 The Moment of Absolute Helplessness [11 Dec 2025]
Kalu was lying there. No dramatic bleeding. No obvious wounds. But his body was wrong. His neck. His head. His eye. He was breathing, but not right.

I asked people to help. I begged them to stop a rickshaw so my wife and I could take him to a hospital. No one helped. People looked. Some spoke. Some moved away. No one acted.

That moment, standing there, surrounded by people, unable to move my injured dog, is one I will never forget.

### 7.5 Bringing Him Home to Save Him [11 Dec 2025]
I ran back home. Took the car. Put Kalu inside. Every movement felt wrong. Every bump on the road felt like damage I could not see. We did not discuss where to go. We just went to the nearest place we knew could help.

### 7.6 Petozone, Pimple Saudagar, The First Right Step [11 Dec 2025]
We reached Petozone, Pimple Saudagar between 10:00 PM and 11:00 PM. They examined him immediately. Externally, there were no major injuries. That almost gave false hope. Internally, they knew it was bad.

They told us clearly: neck injury. Head trauma. Left eye damage. Possible spinal cord involvement.

They did not waste time. They gave saline. They stabilised him as much as possible. They did not give false assurance. They told us the truth: "This is critical. He needs a 24/7 advanced hospital."

We accepted that immediately. There was no argument. No denial. We were ready to fight.

### 7.7 Searching for Care in the Middle of the Night [11 Dec 2025]
From that moment, the night became a series of refusals. We went to Crown Vet, Aundh. They refused us. Not after examination. Not after seeing Kalu. They did not come out. No doctor. No nurse. Only the watchman interacted with us, acting as a mediator between us and a building that had the lights on but the doors closed.

That refusal stayed with me. Not because they could not help, but because they did not even look.

From there, every call, every suggestion pointed to one place: "Raintree." "The best place." "The only place." "24/7."

We called. First refusal, no occupancy. We went there personally. Second refusal, annual anniversary party. A celebration.
We reached Vetic and were admitted between 11:30 PM and 12:00 AM.

## 8.0 The Day We Waited, and the Door Stayed Closed [12 Dec 2025]

### 8.1 Our Account [12 Dec 2025]
12 December 2025 did not begin with panic. It began with hope under exhaustion.

Kalu was still at Vetic. He was injured, severely, but he was alive. That single fact carried us through the night and into the morning. We had not slept properly. We had slept in the car in fragments. Two hours here. Three hours there. But we were awake enough to notice something important. Kalu was responding. He reacted to familiar voices. He lifted his ears. He tried to move. This was not recovery, but it was presence, and presence is all hope needs.

The doctors at Vetic were clear and consistent. They did not sugar-coat anything. They did not speak in circles. They explained Kalu's condition in plain human language, not technical jargon. Someone was continuously monitoring him. We could see that with our own eyes.

Investigations were already done: X-rays, blood tests, monitoring. And we could also see the ceiling. Vetic did not have MRI. They did not have certain advanced interventions. They told us this honestly. That honesty mattered. But it also forced us into a corner.

Every serious conversation that day eventually returned to one word: MRI. Not because MRI guarantees survival. But because without it, no one could fully see what had been damaged, especially with suspected neck, head, and spinal injury.

And in Pune, MRI for animals meant something very specific. It meant one place. There were no alternatives we could choose between. There was no comparison to make. The system narrowed to a single path.

On 12 December, we went personally to Raintree to request admission. Not to argue. Not to complain. To request. To explain. To ask them to take Kalu.

We went there knowing fully that Kalu was in a fragile state, moving him carried risk, but staying without MRI also carried risk. There was no good option. Only responsibility.

That moment stays with me. Not because they refused. But because there was nowhere else to go. There was no second advanced ICU. No backup MRI facility. The reality of monopoly became painfully clear, not as an idea, but as lived experience. When one door controls everything, refusal does not feel like a decision. It feels like being stranded.

We went back to Vetic. Kalu was still there. Alive. Fighting. We stayed near him. Watched him. Waited. We slept in the car again. The night of 12 December passed in suspension, not crisis, not calm. Just waiting for morning.

That night was not dramatic. No alarms. No rushing doctors. Just exhaustion. We kept asking ourselves the same questions silently: Are we doing the right thing? Is moving him tomorrow safe? Is waiting worse? There was no answer. Only time moving forward regardless of our uncertainty.

We knew that 13 December would decide everything. If Raintree took Kalu, the fight would continue inside that system. If they did not, we would be left with no escalation path at all.

That night ended without resolution. But it ended with one plan: We would move Kalu on 13 December. Not because we were confident. But because there was no alternative left.

### 8.2 Clinic Response [12 Dec 2025]
At Raintree, we were told that they could not take Kalu that day. The reasons communicated to us were related to their anniversary event and operational constraints. We were told to come back the next day.

### 8.3 Our Clarification [12 Dec 2025]
This was not a phone call misunderstanding. This was not hearsay. We were there in person, standing outside with a critically injured dog admitted elsewhere, asking for help. The door did not open.

## 9.0 Entering the System With No Way Back [13 Dec 2025]

### 9.1 Our Account [13 Dec 2025]
13 December 2025 began without rest. The night at Vetic had ended in fragments of sleep inside the car, not because we wanted to be close, but because going home felt wrong. Leaving Kalu behind, even for a few hours, felt like abandonment, even though logic said otherwise.

Morning came, and with it came the decision we had already made the night before. We would move Kalu. Not because he was stable. Not because it was safe. But because staying meant accepting limits that could cost him his life.

Inside Vetic, the doctors explained again what we already knew. Kalu was fragile. Neck injury. Head trauma. Possible spinal involvement. Movement itself was a risk.

But they also knew what we knew. Without MRI, without advanced intervention, we were standing still in a situation that demanded motion. There was no drama in that conversation. Only gravity.

When it was time to move Kalu, everything slowed down. Every touch mattered. Every lift felt dangerous. This was not just transferring a patient. This was transferring hope.

We left Vetic knowing that, emotionally, we were leaving the place where we had felt the most clarity and empathy since the accident. That hurt more than we expected.

We left Vetic at 10:30 AM on 13 December and reached Raintree around 11:15 AM. When we reached Raintree, Kalu was brought in. There was no stretcher. Despite clear neck and spinal injury, he was lifted and carried by hand. That image stayed with me. Not because I expected luxury. But because basic caution mattered. CCTV would later show who carried him. But at that moment, there was no space to argue. Only fear that another wrong movement could add damage we would never see.
The admission wait continued around 11:30 AM.

The first thing that shifted was not treatment. It was communication. At Vetic, doctors spoke to us like people. At Raintree, the language became technical immediately. Medical terms. Short explanations. Assumptions of understanding. We kept asking for clarification. Sometimes we received it. Sometimes we were left translating jargon into meaning ourselves. This was not hostility. But it was distance. And distance matters when you are terrified.

Very early on, we were told something that stayed with us. Kalu's condition was critical. The injuries were severe. Survival was not guaranteed. We accepted that. We never denied the seriousness. What we struggled with was how that seriousness was communicated, more as data than as preparation.

MRI did not happen immediately. Not because it was not needed. But because it depended on availability, specifically, the availability of an anaesthesia expert. Time passed. We waited. We followed up. Finally, after 3:30 PM, things began to move. The MRI process was initiated.

Consent for anaesthesia was taken on the spot. There was no long preparation. No gradual explanation over the day.

Kalu was taken for MRI. We waited outside. There is a particular kind of waiting that happens when you have no control, no information, and no alternative. This was that kind. Minutes stretched. Silence felt loud.

When Kalu was brought back and anaesthesia was reversed, something unexpected happened. He responded. He reacted to our voices. He lifted his head. He tried to stand. For a moment, just a moment, it felt like something had shifted. Not recovery. But resilience. That moment mattered more than any report. It reminded us that Kalu was still present, still fighting, still connected.

After MRI, permissions began coming in waves. More tests. Blood work. Tick fever testing from Bangalore. Further monitoring. We did not hesitate. Money was never a discussion. We said yes to everything. Even when they told us he might never walk again. We said yes. We accepted that future without negotiation.

### 9.2 Clinic Response [13 Dec 2025]
We were told clearly: "There is no guarantee he will survive MRI or anaesthesia."

### 9.3 Our Clarification [13 Dec 2025]
That sentence did not come before the process was set in motion. It came inside it. We were given very little time to process it. But we agreed. Because MRI had become the gatekeeper for everything else.

## 10.0 The Night Trust Broke [13-14 Dec 2025]

### 10.1 Our Account [13-14 Dec 2025]
We were home, but we were not present. Our bodies were inside the house, but our minds were still inside the ICU.

It was around 10:45 PM when the call came saying Kalu's condition was not good. We immediately said we would come. There was no hesitation. No discussion. We were told we could not. Visiting hours were over. The explanation was procedural, not medical. That distinction mattered more than anyone realised.

We asked what "not good" meant. The answers were vague. Words like unstable, monitoring, critical floated around without anchors. There was no clear picture, no step-by-step explanation of what had changed since we last saw him at 8:30 PM. We asked if vitals were okay. We were told they were being monitored. We asked again. We were told things were under control. That conversation ended without clarity.

Around 11:30 PM, we called again. The person on the phone, reception, told us: "Kalu is fine. Vitals are normal." That sentence should have ended the anxiety. It did not. Because immediately after that reassurance, we heard shouting in the background. Not raised voices in urgency. Shouting. We could hear staff arguing. We could hear panic. We could hear a machine making repeated beep-beep sounds. No one muted the phone. No one ended the call. We heard everything.

Through the noise, we caught fragments. Someone was shouting about a monitoring device not being attached. There was argument over whose responsibility it was. Voices overlapped. No one was calm. And then the call disconnected.

For a few seconds, we sat frozen. Not because we did not understand what we heard. But because we understood too many possibilities at once. Was that Kalu? Was his monitor disconnected? Was he the dog collapsing in the background? Had we just heard the moment everything went wrong? There was no way to know. And there was no one explaining.

We called back immediately. No answer. We called again. No answer. The minutes stretched. Fear does something strange at moments like this, it creates images you cannot stop. We did not imagine worst cases. We imagined uncertainty.

### 10.2 Clinic Response [13-14 Dec 2025]
At around 12:05 AM, someone finally answered. We asked directly about the shouting and the argument. The response was simple: "That was another dog." No explanation. No context. No reassurance beyond the sentence itself. We were told Kalu was okay. Vitals were fine. We asked again. We were told the same thing.

### 10.3 Our Clarification [13-14 Dec 2025]
We accepted the explanation because we had no choice. But one question stayed with us, unanswered. If that was another dog, what happened to that dog? Did it survive? Did it collapse? Did it die? We were never told. And that silence mattered.

By that point, something had shifted inside us. Until then, we had trusted that whatever was happening inside the ICU was controlled, even if it was serious. After that call, trust became conditional. Not because of intent. Not because of accusation. Because information had fractured.

From that moment on, we started recording calls. Not to accuse. To protect ourselves from not knowing.

That night, sleep did not come. Not because we were waiting for a call. But because our minds were replaying the same sounds. The shouting. The beeping. The abrupt silence. We lay there, counting minutes, checking phones, imagining updates that never came. We were inside rules, but outside care.

Eventually, the night thinned into morning. There was no closure to that call. No follow-up message. No proactive update.

## 11.0 Morning, Clarity, and a New Kind of Panic [14 Dec 2025]

### 11.1 Our Account [14 Dec 2025]
Morning arrived, but it did not bring relief. It brought exposure. The kind that happens when you finally see something in daylight that you were forced to imagine in the dark.

We reached Raintree as early as we could. Not because we expected good news. But because we needed confirmation. After a night filled with fragments, shouting, beeping, silence, seeing Kalu with our own eyes was the only thing that mattered.

Visiting hours began. We were allowed in.

Kalu was there. Alive. That alone steadied us for a moment. But his condition had changed. He looked more tired. More withdrawn. His responses were slower. The spark we had seen after MRI the previous evening felt dimmer. This was not sudden collapse. It was drift. The kind that happens when the body is fighting on too many fronts at once.

We asked what had happened during the night. The answers came in pieces. No single explanation. No clear timeline. Just reassurances layered over concern. Vitals. Monitoring. Stability. Words that sound safe, but do not explain progression.

We asked about the shouting we heard on the call. The explanation remained the same: "It was another dog." No additional detail. No acknowledgement of how terrifying that sounded to us as parents waiting at home. The topic moved on quickly.

As discussions continued, something else became clear. Most explanations were framed in technical terms. Values. Indicators. Clinical phrasing. We tried to keep up. But when you are emotionally exhausted, jargon becomes a barrier, not information.

At one point, my sister asked about moving Kalu to another place. The response from the main doctor that morning, Dr. Leila Fernandez, was restrained, almost detached. It was not hostility. It was fatigue. She mentioned that moving him was possible, but she did not encourage it.

I knew then that Kalu was not in a condition to be moved again. I said clearly that we would not be taking him anywhere else. As his owner, that responsibility was mine.

Later, after continued questions, the same doctor explained Kalu's condition again, this time in simpler language. Not because the facts had changed. But because we needed meaning, not numbers. That explanation brought brief relief. Not hope. Understanding.

Around noon, the conversation shifted. Kalu had developed clotting issues. We were told he needed fresh plasma urgently. There was no delay in urgency. But there was delay in solution. We were told that we would need to arrange a donor dog.

The criteria were strict: vaccinated dog, weight 25 kg or more, no history of tick fever, not on antibiotics or other medications. We did not have such a dog. The clinic's known donor dogs were already exhausted from previous donations. There was no blood bank to draw from. There was no ready plasma available.

What was urgent medically became logistically impossible.

We stepped outside. Phones came out immediately. We started calling: friends, friends of friends, NGOs, WhatsApp groups, Instagram, Facebook. We posted messages everywhere. Time did not slow down for effort. The more urgent the need became, the more clearly we saw how fragile the system was. There was no central coordination. No fallback. Just desperate outreach.

Finally, a woman we had never met, Lady X, agreed to help. She was coming from far away with her dog. That yes felt unreal. Relief mixed with fear. Would the dog meet criteria? Would the blood match? Would time run out before she arrived? We waited.

We were told he needed fresh plasma urgently. We were told that we would need to arrange a donor dog.

### 11.3 Our Clarification [14 Dec 2025]
What was urgent medically became logistically impossible.

## 12.0 When Help Arrived, But Time Did Not Wait [15 Dec 2025]

### 12.1 Our Account [15 Dec 2025]
The morning of 15 December 2025 did not begin with questions. It began with fatigue. Not the kind that sleep fixes. The kind that settles into bones after days of alertness without rest.

We reached Raintree again, carrying the weight of the previous night's pause. Lady X had already done more than anyone could reasonably ask. And yet, everything still depended on what the system could do next.

We asked about the blood sample taken the previous night. The responses were unclear. Someone mentioned platelets. Someone mentioned mismatch. Someone said something did not align. There was no single explanation that connected all the pieces. Information came indirectly, sometimes to my sister, sometimes to us, and often without context. Confusion grew not because things were complex, but because they were not explained cleanly.

Later that day, when Dr. Leila Fernandez reviewed the situation again, the message changed. We were told that Lady X's dog was suitable, and that further blood extraction could proceed.

The requirements were laid out clearly: the donor dog should be fasting, no food for 4 hours, no water for 2 hours before extraction. We contacted Lady X again. She was working. She rearranged her day. Her husband helped. She came back. This mattered more to us than any protocol.

Lady X's dog was sedated. Blood was extracted. We watched this with a mix of gratitude and fear. Gratitude for a stranger's kindness. Fear that even this might not be enough.

We asked repeatedly for the clinic to post donor requests on their own platforms. They did, but with delay. By then, we had already mobilised on our own.

As hours passed, it became clear that backup was necessary. If Lady X's blood did not work, there would be no time to start again. So we kept calling.

By the end of the day, around 10 dogs were contacted or arranged. Some were physically brought. Some sent samples via nearby clinics. Some did not meet weight criteria. Some did not match. Some were ruled out for medical reasons.

This was not a process. It was crowdsourced survival.

Lady X's dog's blood was extracted around 2:30 PM. The plan was plasma separation. But there was a problem. There was no centrifuge available for plasma separation. We were told that natural separation would be attempted, a process that takes hours.

We waited. Time passed. After six hours, we were told that the natural process was not working.

My wife has experience in pathology. She suggested finding an external centrifuge machine at a known lab or blood bank to separate plasma. We raised this possibility. One of the main doctors acknowledged that it could work. We started looking immediately. But time was slipping. The blood had been extracted at noon. The usable window was narrowing.

Eventually, a decision was made. Instead of plasma alone, they would attempt a direct blood transfusion. We agreed. At this point, agreement was not optimism. It was necessity.

We were told the transfusion would take around four hours. We were told it would be stopped if Kalu's temperature rose.

At around 8:30 PM, the blood transfusion began. We waited. Every minute felt like negotiation with fate.

At around 10:30 PM, we were informed that Kalu's temperature had risen. The transfusion was stopped. There was no argument. There was no protest. This was protocol. And protocol had now replaced hope.

That night, the language shifted again. The tone became heavier. Words like poor prognosis began to surface more openly. We were told that we might need to consider a final decision. We did not accept that yet. Not because we were in denial. But because Kalu was still alive.

By the end of 15 December, we were no longer fighting for recovery. We were fighting for time. Time to understand. Time to decide. Time to be present.

We even discussed, quietly, the possibility of taking Kalu to his favourite place one last time before letting him go peacefully at a regular vet. That thought alone felt unbearable. But it entered the room.

Night fell without resolution.

## 13.0 The Day He Left [16 Dec 2025]

### 13.1 Our Account [16 Dec 2025]
The morning of 16 December 2025 arrived without negotiation. There was no sense of maybe today will be better. There was no false optimism left. There was only presence.

When we reached Raintree that morning, Kalu was still alive. But his body was no longer holding itself together. The signs were visible, even before anyone explained them. His paws were swollen. His abdomen was distended. His urine was red, showing internal bleeding.

We were told that his liver and kidneys were failing. The words multiple organ failure were no longer implied, they were spoken. This was no longer about one injury. It was systemic.

The staff adjusted his position. They spread his legs gently to help with breathing. They tried to reduce pressure on his lungs and spine. This was not treatment anymore. This was comfort. And that distinction was devastating.

At some point that morning, the conversation everyone dreads entered the room. We were told that we might need to consider letting Kalu go peacefully. Not as a recommendation framed gently over time, but as a possibility that had arrived suddenly.

We listened. We did not argue. But we were not ready. Not because we did not understand the reality. But because Kalu was still there. He was still breathing. He was still warm. He was still present. As long as that was true, we wanted everything possible to be done. We said that clearly.

Around 12:00 PM, Kalu stopped breathing. There was no warning alarm. No gradual slowdown. It happened suddenly. One moment there was movement. The next, there was not.

CPR was initiated immediately. Protocols were followed. Intubation. Chest compressions. Manual air pumping with an ambu-bag. Kalu responded briefly. For a moment, it felt like he came back. That moment gave false hope. Within 10 to 15 minutes, his body gave out again. This time, there was no return.

I was watching his face. Not the monitors. His eyes did not move. His chest did not rise on its own. There was no response, no reflex, no awareness. I said it out loud. "He is gone." I looked at the assigned vet, Anushree, and said it again. She acknowledged it.

And yet, the ambu-bag was still being pressed. Air was still being pushed into a body that had already let go. I asked her to stop. Only then was the bag removed.

What followed was not immediate closure. Tubes were not removed right away. Monitoring equipment stayed attached longer than it should have. Kalu lay there, still connected, still medicalized, even though life had already left. That image is difficult to erase.

There was no preparation. No soft transition. No emotional grounding. No one spoke to us about what would happen next. There was no moment of shared silence. Only procedure.

We cleared the bills without dispute. Advance payments had already been taken daily. Money felt irrelevant at that point. It was not about cost. It was about ending with dignity, and that had already passed.

We took Kalu to the cremation facility near Naidu Hospital, Sangamwadi. We called everyone who loved him. Friends. Family. Those who had known him as more than a dog. We performed the rituals. We stayed until the end.

When we returned home, the house felt unfamiliar. Not empty. Unrecognizable. No footsteps. No movement. No presence.

Night came again. And for the first time in nine years, there was no reason to open the door after 9 PM. No night walk. No return.

Kalu did not leave quietly. He fought. We fought with him. We accept that he may not have survived anywhere. But survival is not the only measure of care. Empathy matters. Communication matters. Preparedness matters. And when systems fail, the weight falls entirely on those who love.

For Kalu.

## 14.0 What Stayed, What Broke, and Why This Exists [16 Dec 2025]

### 14.1 The First Days After [16 Dec 2025]
After 16 December 2025, there was no next step. There was no treatment plan. No follow-up call. No "what happens now." There was only absence.

The days immediately after Kalu's passing were not dramatic. They were empty. Not the kind of emptiness that screams, the kind that settles quietly into every corner of the house. Doors stayed closed longer. Lights stayed off more. Sounds felt unnecessary. Night came without purpose.

For nine years, night had meant something. A walk. A return. Movement. Now it meant nothing.

### 14.2 Grief Did Not Arrive All at Once [16 Dec 2025]
Grief came in fragments. Sometimes as anger. Sometimes as guilt. Sometimes as numbness. Not all at the hospital. Not all at the accident. Often at small, unexpected moments. Opening the door and expecting movement. Hearing a sound and turning instinctively. Reaching for a routine that no longer existed. Grief did not ask for permission.

### 14.3 The Questions That Would Not Leave [16 Dec 2025]
With time, grief stopped being loud and became persistent. Questions surfaced, not because we wanted someone to blame, but because things did not add up.

Why was there no stretcher? Why did availability matter more than urgency? Why was empathy absent when outcome was uncertain? Why did communication fail when clarity was most needed? Why did everything depend on one place, one system, one bottleneck?

These were not questions asked in anger. They were questions asked because we did everything we were told to do. And still, we were left feeling uninformed, unprepared, and emotionally abandoned.

### 14.4 Accepting the Outcome, Not the Experience [16 Dec 2025]
We accepted that Kalu may not have survived anywhere. That acceptance came early. What did not sit right was the experience. The lack of guidance. The lack of emotional preparation. The reliance on rigid systems during human moments.

Critical care is not only about machines. It is about how people are treated when they are already breaking.

### 14.5 Hearing Other Stories [16 Dec 2025]
In the days that followed, we spoke to other pet parents. Quietly. Privately. Some shared similar experiences. Some chose not to speak publicly. We do not speak on their behalf. But hearing them confirmed one thing: What we experienced was not isolated.

### 14.6 Why We Chose to Write [16 Dec 2025]
This website, this archive, this record, it is not revenge. It is not an attack. It exists for three reasons:

To honour Kalu's life, not just his death.

To document a real critical-care journey, without exaggeration.

To help other pet parents prepare, emotionally and practically, before emergencies happen.

Silence protects systems. Truth protects people.

### 14.7 What This Is, and What It Is Not [16 Dec 2025]
This is not a call to boycott. Not a demand for punishment. Not a denial of medical complexity.

It is a request for better communication, better empathy, better preparedness, better alternatives in a city that deserves more than one option, especially when lives depend on it.

### 14.8 Responsibility Without Regret [16 Dec 2025]
We do not regret fighting. We do not regret spending. We do not regret insisting. We would do it all again. Because Kalu fought. And when someone you love is fighting, you do not calculate outcomes, you show up.

### 14.9 Kalu's Legacy [16 Dec 2025]
Kalu's life was not defined by his end. He lived freely. He loved deeply. He adapted constantly. He held a family together when it was fragile. He chose people when it mattered. He trusted us with his life.

This archive exists because that trust deserves respect.

### 14.10 If You Are Reading This as a Pet Parent [16 Dec 2025]
Please understand this: emergencies remove choice. Systems matter. Preparation matters. Ask questions before you need answers. Know your options before panic narrows them. Demand clarity before exhaustion takes it away. And never feel ashamed for asking for empathy.

For Kalu.

Kalu was not perfect. He was not easy. He was not replaceable. He was family. A small brother. A constant presence. A life that mattered. This is for him.

## 15.0 Learnings for Pet Parents

### 15.1 Before an Emergency
- Save 3 contacts: nearest 24/7 ICU, nearest trauma-capable clinic, emergency transport option.
- Ask upfront: ICU availability, imaging flow, blood/plasma process, donor steps, visiting policy.

### 15.2 During Admission
- Ask for a written summary: current status + key risks + monitoring plan.
- Ask who is the night point-of-contact and how often updates will come.

### 15.3 During Imaging (MRI/CT)
- Ask: where it happens, transport method, anesthesia plan, expected report timeline.
- Ask: what happens if the specialist/report is delayed.

### 15.4 During Transfusion
- Ask: trigger criteria, alternatives, monitoring protocol, risks, and reaction handling.

### 15.5 Communication Standards to Expect
- Clear and simple explanations.
- Defined update frequency.
- Clear next steps for the next 6 to 12 hours.
