Kalu‚Äôs life with us

Kalu, our indie dog, looked like a Border Collie and found us on 26 Nov 2016 on the road near our house.

He was a charming, smart boy ‚Äî always understanding, never troubled, never broke anything. He understood more than 40‚Äì50 Marathi words and was closely attached to our family.

We never chained him because we had a big flat and a big garden space behind. He never asked for dog food. He liked milk, bread, and chicken tandoor on weekends, and recently started enjoying Drools and Choco-cos, but he was never demanding.

We went through many difficult situations at home, including financial issues ‚Äî he was always emotional support and truly a family member.

We never liked bikes or mopeds, but recently he started enjoying car rides. He was ready whenever we took car keys for office, and we used to give him small rides or take him to multiple places.

It felt like he started enjoying freedom due to our new car and the stability we recently got.

He recently celebrated his 9th birthday on 26 Nov at Ladghar Beach. It was my dad‚Äôs wish to take him to the beach; even though he was scared of water (even for bath), he enjoyed the beach and the vibes.

But the day of the accident came on 11 Dec.

As usual we left him for walk as daily routine ‚Äî he goes, takes his walk and comes back. But this day was different. I received a call from a friend about his accident by drunk drivers on two wheelers. They flew away. We took Kalu to Petozone, then Vetic Kalyani Nagar, then Raintree, Ghorpadi.

Accident and early response

Kalu had no external wounds, but major internal injuries involving the head, neck, left eye, spinal cord, lungs, liver, kidneys, bladder, and internal bleeding. We fully accept his condition was critical and life-threatening.

On 11 December, a friend called us about his accident. It was caused by drunk drivers on two wheelers who fled the spot.

Initial stabilization was done at Petozone Clinic, Pimple Saudagar, and we were advised to move to a 24/7 advanced hospital.

Crown Vet, Aundh, did not even come out to see our dog. Only a watchman interacted with us as a mediator.

Multiple vets told us Raintree is the only place in Pune with advanced facilities like MRI and 24/7 ICU. We reached Raintree because there was no real choice.

Our dog was admitted to Vetic, Kalyani Nagar for two days (11‚Äì12 December). They did immediate treatment like X-ray and blood tests. We saw some improvement, but they did not have next-level treatment like lung fluid removal and MRI. We had to sleep in the car and barely got 2‚Äì3 hours of rest. The doctors there were empathic, explained things in normal words, and kept continued monitoring by one person near our dog.

On 12 December, we personally approached Raintree for emergency admission. First we were told there was no occupancy. Later, we were refused due to their annual anniversary event. This was the first major red flag.

Day 1 ‚Äì 13 December (Admission & MRI)

Admission & handling

Our dog Kalu was admitted on 13 December at Raintree.

Despite neck and spinal injuries, no stretcher was used. He was lifted by hand, which risked further damage.

MRI & anesthesia

MRI urgency was not taken up immediately. It happened only after repeated follow-ups and when the anesthesia expert was available.

The MRI was scheduled after around 3:30 PM because the anesthesia expert was available only then.

Consent for anesthesia was taken on the spot, without proper prior explanation.

We were informed after sedation that there was no guarantee Kalu would survive anesthesia.

MRI and anesthesia were handled by Dr. Muskan.

Raintree used a nearby MRI center within about 1 km of the hospital.

After reversal, Kalu responded to us, lifted his ears, opened his eyes, and tried to stand. We felt hope.

After MRI

We agreed to every test, without thinking about money.

We were told Kalu may never walk again, and we accepted full responsibility for lifelong care.

Tick fever tests were sent outside Pune.

Night of 13 December ‚Äì Serious trust-breaking moment

We left around 10:00 PM after seeing Kalu responsive and alert.

At 10:45 PM, we received a call saying his condition was not good.

We offered to come immediately, but we were told we are not allowed to see him due to visiting-hour rules.

Later that night, we were told Kalu was stable.

During the same phone call, we clearly heard shouting, alarms, and staff arguing about monitoring equipment not being attached to a dog.

The call ended suddenly.

We recorded the call because we were becoming suspicious about the handling.

Later, we were told it was ‚Äúsome other dog‚Äù.

At 12:05 AM our calls were not picked. When they finally responded, they said our dog was okay.

As dog parents, hearing this while our own dog was critical was terrifying and disturbing.

We still do not know which dog it was or what happened.

Day 2 ‚Äì 14 December (Blood & plasma crisis)

Communication & empathy

Most explanations were given in technical medical language.

Only when Dr. Leila Fernandez explained in simple words did we clearly understand Kalu‚Äôs condition.

The assigned vet Anushree showed no empathy, no emotional support, and constant negativity.

We were told we could move Kalu elsewhere, but he was not in a condition to travel long distance.

Plasma emergency

Kalu urgently required fresh plasma due to clotting issues.

Raintree had no plasma separation centrifuge and no blood bank support.

We were told to arrange donor dogs ourselves with strict criteria.

Criteria: vaccinated, 25 kg+, no history of tick fever, and no antibiotics or ongoing medication.

We contacted NGOs, friends, Instagram, WhatsApp, and Facebook groups.

Lady X, a complete stranger, came from far with her dog purely out of kindness.

Even then, sampling was delayed and cross-matching was pushed to the next morning due to lab availability.

We asked the hospital to share a donor post on their page; it was posted only after a delay.

This was emotionally and physically draining for a family already breaking down.

Day 3 ‚Äì 15 December (MRI report delay & transfusion)

MRI report delay

MRI report was not shared because the neurologist was unavailable.

Only after Tata Small Animals Hospital, Mumbai, contacted the same doctor on our behalf did the report arrive.

Until then, information was unclear and incomplete.

Reports on the Vet Buddy portal were also delayed; we had to request uploads repeatedly.

This made us feel that external pressure was needed to receive basic medical clarity.

Blood transfusion

Plasma separation failed due to lack of equipment.

Lady X‚Äôs dog was kept fasting (food for 4 hours, water for 2 hours) before blood extraction.

The collected blood was kept for natural plasma separation, but it did not work in time.

We searched for a centrifuge option; Dr. Khambta said it could work, but time was running out.

The plan changed to direct blood transfusion.

We were told Kalu had liver and kidney issues, bladder urine was red due to internal injuries, and his paws were swelling.

We had arranged nearly 10 dogs; some didn‚Äôt match, some didn‚Äôt fit weight criteria.

Some people offered to send blood samples through porter and dunzo with ice packs.

Transfusion was stopped midway as Kalu‚Äôs temperature increased.

We were told it would take 4 hours, and they would stop if his temperature rose. It was stopped after about 30 minutes.

Day 4 ‚Äì 16 December (Kalu passed away)

Kalu stopped breathing around 12 PM.

CPR was attempted.

Even after clear signs that he was gone, manual air pumping continued, which was extremely distressing to witness as pet parents.

We asked them to stop the air pumping. The balloon-like tube was removed later, but other monitoring equipment was not removed immediately.

We had been asked for daily advance payments of 25k; the bill was always above that amount.

We cleared all bills without any argument.

We took Kalu for cremation near Naidu Hospital, Sangamwadi, called his loved ones, performed our rituals, and said our final goodbye.

Final words for pet parents

We accept that our dog Kalu may not have survived anywhere.

We were ready to fight for him with time, effort, and money, without hesitation.

But how a dog is handled, spoken about, and supported while fighting for life matters deeply. Empathy, clear communication, preparedness, and honest guidance matter ‚Äî especially when a hospital holds a monopoly and pet parents have no alternative.

This review is not written in anger. It is written so no other pet parent feels as helpless as we did.
